Analytics

Analytics dashboard for autonomous cleaning robots fleet management

Analytics

Accessed via LionsCloud, a B2B robotics fleet management platform, the analytics dashboard allows internal stakeholders, distributors, and end-users of autonomous cleaning robots to get real-time, at-a-glance insights of their fleet performance.

This dashboard showcases key performance indicators such as area cleaned, cleaning efficiency, and error reports. These metrics enable users to track return on investment, make data-driven decisions, and optimize the utilization of their autonomous cleaning robots.

Problem statement

Analysis of customer needs reveals a critical gap in our current system.

Users lack comprehensive insights into their robots' performance, struggle to identify reasons for suboptimal operation, and have limited visibility into encountered errors. This lack of information could erode customer confidence and lead to frustration.

“How might we empower distributors through LionsCloud to gain quick insights into robot performance and effectively diagnose errors?”

Design Exploration

Kickstarting the design

Having identified an analytics dashboard as the strategic solution to address our problem statements, I initiated the design process by breaking down the key metrics that are important to each user group.

Each metric was then represented by a card design.

Design strategy: Bento above the fold

Each user role was envisioned to access a tailored analytics view, with metric cards strategically grouped to align with their specific responsibilities and operational needs.

To optimise user attention and prevent cognitive overload, the design adheres to 2 key principles:

  • Max of 7 cards
  • All key info must be above the fold for immediate visibility and at-a-glance monitoring

Initial tests and iterations

To ensure rapid development, the Analytics page was to be rolled out in phases/sprints, with phase 1 delivering an MVP focused on technicians - our primary user group. Initial user tests were then carried out these users.

Search Bar

Feedback from users revealed a need for a search bar. Technicians typically look for robots via the robot ID or its company. As such, a search bar was added to allow quick narrowing down of data to the ones of interest.

Date ranges

Date range selectors were also added to cards to allow viewing of data from specific time period

Filters

Subsequent tests shown that having just 1 search bar is insufficient as it can only query one item at a time. Users also wanted to drill down the data via other filter categories like company, worksites and robot type.

As such, filters in the form of side drawers were added. The date range selectors in the cards were also tucked into the drawer to declutter the page.

More details

Based on user feedback seeking more granular information, the analytics dashboard was enhanced with a side drawer displaying mission details for each robot, including metrics like coverage area and duration.

Design Reviews and more iterations

The wireframes were then converted to HI-FI for design reviews with stakeholders, both internal and external.

Following feedback, iterative design and testing, key changes include further simplification of design and finalising of key indicators to be shown in the dashboard.

Changing of Key indicators

Rather than highlighting error counts, we refocused on performance targets - a metric that would be useful across all user groups. This shift allows users to track robots against their defined cleaning goals, such as area cleaned or cleaning hours.

Other key indicators were also swapped with data that was existing in the system, enabling faster MVP development without compromising user needs.

Filter interaction

User testing further revealed usability issues with the side drawer filters. Users often apply filters iteratively to narrow down their search, making the drawer mechanism cumbersome for exploratory filtering.

Side drawer filters - High interaction cost

Increased number of clicks to obtain a desired result as the filters are not instant or dynamic.

UX improvement: Dynamic Filters

Filters were shifted out to the main page, allowing users to dynamically and instantly filter and check the results.

Scrolling interaction

Usability issues with scrolling within the filters were also identified. Long lists in each filter category led to internal scrolling within each category, causing a double-scrolling effect that negatively impacted the filter experience.

Scroll within scroll

To be able to scroll through the list of filters, users need to know how to position their mouse such that they can scroll the main list of filters instead of within each filter category.

UX improvement: Dynamic Filters

A max of 5 items for each filter category is displayed. No scrolling in each category is allowed. Users can click on ‘View all’ to be brought to a dedicated list of all items in that category.

MVP

To further reduce development time, the design had to be further phased out. Graphs and charts which were deemed as lower priority, were to be shifted to future development sprints. In return, the dynamic filter interaction was kept.

Closing thoughts

Early collaboration with developers and stakeholders proved essential in streamlining work and shaping a focused MVP. However, design does not stop here.

The current Analytics feature offers a solid foundation with the potential to incorporate many more indicators over time. As the analytics page evolves with each sprint, the design process remains adaptive and ongoing, continually enhancing the product’s value to users.
